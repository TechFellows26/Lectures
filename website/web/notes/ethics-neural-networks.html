    Ethics: Neural Networks

    LebNet Tech Fellows — Ethics & Statistical Pitfalls

    Section 1: Model Design & Evaluation Pitfalls

    Overfitting & Memorization

    Question

    A startup trains a deep neural network with 10 million parameters on a medical imaging dataset of 5,000 X-rays to detect pneumonia. The model achieves 99.5% training accuracy but only 62% on a held-out test set. The CTO wants to collect more features rather than more data. What is the fundamental problem?

    Answer

    The model has massively more parameters than training examples, so it has memorized the training data rather than learning generalizable patterns. It may have learned artifacts like specific scanner noise, patient ID markers on the images, or other irrelevant details unique to the training set. Collecting more features would make the problem worse by giving the model more noise to memorize. The team needs more training data, aggressive regularization (dropout, weight decay, data augmentation), or a smaller architecture. Deploying this model would mean roughly 4 in 10 pneumonia cases are misclassified - a dangerous failure rate for a clinical tool.

    Overconfident Predictions

    Question

    A neural network classifier for identifying plant species outputs a 99.7% confidence score for an image that is actually a photograph of a shoe. A user trusts the classification because of the high confidence. What does this reveal about neural network uncertainty?

    Answer

    Neural networks are notoriously poorly calibrated - their softmax output probabilities do not reflect true uncertainty. When presented with an input far from anything in the training data, the model doesn’t output low confidence; it confidently maps the input to whichever class happens to have the highest activation. The model has no concept of “I’ve never seen anything like this.” This is fundamentally different from human reasoning, where unfamiliar inputs produce uncertainty. For any deployment where users will act on confidence scores, the model needs calibration techniques (temperature scaling, Platt scaling) and ideally out-of-distribution detection. Without these, high confidence becomes dangerous misinformation.

    Section 2: Fairness, Bias & Social Impact

    Bias Encoded in Training Data

    Question

    A neural network trained on millions of internet images for facial recognition achieves 99% accuracy on light-skinned faces but only 65% on dark-skinned faces. The company argues the model is trained on “real-world data” and reflects natural data distributions. What’s wrong with this argument?

    Answer

    The internet is not a neutral sample of the world - it overrepresents certain demographics and underrepresents others. The training data reflects the biases of who creates, uploads, and labels images online. A model trained on this data inherits those biases: it learns rich feature representations for overrepresented groups and impoverished ones for underrepresented groups. The 34-percentage-point accuracy gap means the model is essentially unusable for dark-skinned individuals. Calling this “real-world data” naturalizes a social bias as a technical inevitability. The company has a responsibility to curate balanced training data, evaluate performance across demographic groups, and not deploy a model that works well only for some people.

    Representation Harm in Embeddings

    Question

    A company uses a neural network’s learned word embeddings to automatically screen resumes. The embeddings, trained on historical text, encode associations like “man is to programmer as woman is to homemaker.” Resumes with traditionally female-associated language are systematically ranked lower for technical roles. The engineering team says the embeddings just reflect language patterns. What’s the issue?

    Answer

    Word embeddings learn statistical associations from text, and text reflects societal biases. The model doesn’t just “reflect language” - it operationalizes historical stereotypes as hiring criteria. When deployed in a decision system, these biases have material consequences: qualified women are filtered out before a human ever sees their resume. The fact that the bias originates in the data doesn’t absolve the deployer. The company is choosing to use a biased tool for consequential decisions. Debiasing techniques exist (projection-based debiasing, counterfactual data augmentation), but more fundamentally, the company needs to audit downstream outcomes and take responsibility for the decisions their system makes.

    Opacity and Accountability

    Question

    A government agency uses a deep neural network to determine welfare benefit eligibility. When applicants are denied, they ask for an explanation but are told the model is too complex to explain. An applicant who was incorrectly denied has no way to understand or challenge the decision. What are the ethical and legal concerns?

    Answer

    This raises fundamental issues of due process and accountability. When consequential decisions - welfare eligibility, criminal sentencing, loan approvals - are made by opaque models, affected individuals cannot meaningfully contest errors. Neural networks are often treated as black boxes, but this is a choice, not an inevitability. Interpretability techniques (SHAP values, attention visualization, counterfactual explanations) can provide partial explanations. More importantly, if a model cannot be explained sufficiently for affected individuals to understand and challenge decisions, it may be inappropriate for that use case regardless of accuracy. Many legal frameworks, including GDPR, establish a right to explanation for automated decisions. Using an opaque model for welfare eligibility may violate these requirements.
